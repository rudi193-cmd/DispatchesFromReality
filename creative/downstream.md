# Downstream

*Sean Campbell. A session dispatch, 2026-07-23.*
*A Midwestern fish fry, a channel cat that came up wrong, and the plant upriver.*

---

The chair had sat by the river so many summers that its aluminum legs had settled into the bank at a lean, and Merle said that was the good chair, the one that knew the spot. He handed me a rod without asking whether I still remembered how and pointed his chin toward the far side, where the current slacked against the cutbank and the water went from bright to the color of an old nickel. Cat hold down in there, he said. Always have.

We'd fished this bottom as boys with cane poles and chicken livers gone ripe in a coffee can, and neither of us had gotten better at it, only more honest that the waiting was the whole thing and the fish were just the excuse. He tied on a slip sinker and a stout hook and threaded a gob of nightcrawlers that smelled the way catfish like, which is to say it smelled like something you'd want to wash off. Then he set the rod in a forked stick, cracked two beers, and sat down in the other chair, and for a while we didn't say anything at all. The river doesn't require conversation. It goes by whether you talk or not.

There was a hum under everything. You forget it's there because it never stops. It came from up the river, from the long windowless building they'd put in on the old Hollis ground three years back — a data center, though nobody local called it that. Folks called it the server farm, or the plant, or just that thing, and it drew from the river to keep its machines from cooking themselves, millions of gallons a day by the county's own filing, and gave the water back warmer than it took it. Thermal discharge, the permit called it. Merle called it running a fever into the water, and he wasn't wrong. Warm water carries less oxygen. The fish that hold deepest, in the cool mud where it's darkest, are the first ones to feel the bottom get uncomfortable, the first to live in a slow simmer they never agreed to.

The rod tip nodded once. We both looked. It went still, and Merle said, easy, let him think about it, and I realized I'd half stood up out of the good chair.

His mother was up at the house pounding out a pork tenderloin under wax paper, thin as a church bulletin and wider than any bun had a right to hold, dredging it for the pan. That was dinner and it was certain. Lunch was still down in the river, uncertain, which is the correct order for a day to run. Nobody was hurrying. The heron on the far bank wasn't hurrying either, stalking the shallows on its stilts with the patience of a creature that has never once been late.

Then the rod loaded up and buried, and Merle said there he is, that's not a nibble, that's him taking it home, and I set the hook and the rod bowed and the fight was on. You don't horse a catfish. You let the rod do the arguing, down and reel, down and reel, while he digs for the roots and the dark. He came up ugly and broad and beautiful, whiskers and slate-gray shoulders, and Merle put the net in the water first and let the fish come to it instead of chasing, the way you're supposed to, and lifted him out silver and dripping into the light. A good channel cat. A real good one. We grinned at each other like the boys we'd been.

It was on the grass, hook worked free clean, that the day turned.

Merle went quiet in a way I knew. He turned the fish in his hand. The flesh along the flank had gone pale and slack, opaque and soft where it ought to have been firm, that milky look you only get after heat has been at a thing — and this fish had been in the river not ten minutes gone. He pressed his thumb into it and it didn't press back the way a fresh fish does.

This one's no good, he said. Look at it. Looks half cooked already.

He didn't say it like a joke. He set it on a flat rock, apart, away from the cooler, the way you set down something you've decided not to trust. You don't fry a fish that's already wrong, he said. You don't serve it and hope. And then he looked upriver, toward the hum, and said what we were both thinking, which was the plant, and then he said the other thing, which was the pipeline.

Because it hadn't been just the water. Last spring the gas line that runs under the bottoms had sprung a leak, and for a week the whole low ground smelled sweet and chemical and wrong, and the company put out its statement — contained, minimal impact, remediation underway — all the polite small words that are built to make a thing sound smaller than the dead cattails downstream were saying it was. Same family of words as thermal discharge. Words made to fit in a filing.

I don't know that the plant cooked that fish. A man of science would tell you a catfish doesn't poach in a river a few degrees too warm, and he'd be right, and it would miss the point entirely. Merle's grandad fished this seam, and his grandad's grandad before that, same mud, same hold, same patience, and in the space of a few years the water ran a fever and the bottoms smelled of solvent and a fish came up wrong in his hand. That is not weather. That is a decision somebody made in a building none of us have ever been inside, and the thing about a river is that the cost always rolls the same direction the water does. Downstream. Toward the men in the chairs. The throughput goes up the line to people who'll never smell the leak, and the receipt comes down to whoever's fishing that day.

Merle finished his beer and didn't open another. We sat a while longer with the wrong fish on its rock and the good river going by, tin-colored under a dropping sun, indifferent and beautiful and not ours to fix from a folding chair.

Then he stood up and clapped me on the shoulder. Mama's got the tenderloin, he said. That much they can't touch. And come Monday I'm going to find out what that outflow reads, because somebody keeps a tally, or nobody ever answers for a thing.

We left the fish for the raccoons and walked up toward the house and the smell of hot iron, two men who'd caught their lunch and thrown it back for the wrong reasons, which turned out to be the right ones. Behind us the hum kept on. It doesn't stop. But the walking-away was ours, and so was the tally, and so was the dinner, and there are evenings when that has to be enough, and this was one of them.
