# Arguing with AI: Climate Evidence Debate

**Grade Level:** 6–8
**Subject:** Science / ELA crossover
**Language:** English
**AI Tool Used:** Claude or ChatGPT
**Duration:** 60 minutes (or two 30-min sessions)
**Contributor:** Emerging Rule Team

---

## Objective

Students will evaluate AI-generated arguments about climate science, distinguish between evidence-based claims and unsupported opinions, and practice constructing their own evidence-backed responses.

---

## Standards Alignment

- NGSS MS-ESS3-5 — Ask questions to clarify evidence of factors that cause climate change
- CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.RST.6-8.8 — Distinguish between evidence-based vs. non-evidence-based claims

---

## Materials

- Device with AI chatbot access (1 per pair)
- "Claim Tracker" graphic organizer (template below)
- Access to a trusted reference: NASA Climate Kids, NOAA, or IPCC summary

---

## Lesson Plan

### Warm-Up (10 min)

Pose this question: *"If you asked an AI whether climate change is real, do you think it would always give you accurate information? Why or why not?"*

Brief pair-share, then discuss as a class.

### Main Activity (40 min)

**Part 1 — Interview the AI (15 min)**

Pairs ask the AI two rounds of questions:

> **Round 1 prompt:** "Explain three pieces of scientific evidence that support the conclusion that Earth's climate is warming due to human activity."

> **Round 2 prompt:** "Now give me the strongest argument someone who doubts climate science might make, and then explain what scientists say in response."

Students fill in their Claim Tracker as they read.

**Claim Tracker:**
| Claim | Evidence given? (Y/N) | Source mentioned? | I agree / disagree / unsure |
|---|---|---|---|
| | | | |
| | | | |
| | | | |

**Part 2 — Fact-Check One Claim (15 min)**

Each pair picks ONE claim from the AI and looks it up using NASA, NOAA, or another teacher-approved source. Does the real source support what the AI said?

**Part 3 — Push Back on the AI (10 min)**

> **Sample prompt:** "You said [claim]. But I found that [what you discovered]. Can you correct or clarify your answer?"

Observe how the AI responds. Does it update? Does it get defensive? Does it add new information?

### Wrap-Up / Discussion (10 min)

- What did the AI get right? What was missing or vague?
- What makes a scientific claim trustworthy?
- Should students use AI for science research? What rules would you set?

---

## Assessment

Exit ticket (3 sentences):
1. One AI claim that was well-supported by evidence
2. One AI claim that needed more support or was unclear
3. One rule you'd give a younger student about using AI for science

---

## Differentiation

- **Support:** Pre-fill the Claim Tracker with the AI's claims; students only evaluate
- **Extension:** Students write a 1-page "AI Audit Report" grading the AI's scientific accuracy
- **ELL:** Run Part 1 prompt in Spanish; compare whether the AI's Spanish response differs from the English one

---

## Notes from the Classroom

Students are often surprised when the AI hedges or says "scientists disagree" on questions where there is actually strong consensus. That moment — "wait, why is the AI being wishy-washy about this?" — is a rich entry point into discussing scientific consensus vs. public debate.
