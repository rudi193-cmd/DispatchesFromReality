# Neva and Theo: A Story About Simple Machines

**Lesson Title:** Neva and Theo: A Story About Simple Machines
**Grade Level:** 3–5
**Subject:** Science
**Language:** English
**AI Tool Used:** Claude
**Duration:** 45–60 minutes
**Contributor:** rudi193-cmd

---

## Objective

Students will be able to:
- Identify a ramp (inclined plane) and a lever as simple machines
- Describe how a ramp reduces the force needed to move an object by increasing the distance traveled
- Describe how a lever multiplies force by adjusting the position of the fulcrum
- Explain, in their own words, what mechanical advantage means — after experiencing it with their hands

## Materials

**For the ramp experiment:**
- 1 heavy book or flat object with some weight
- 1 surface to tilt (another book, a piece of cardboard, a folder)
- 1 small object to prop the ramp up (eraser, pencil, block)

**For the lever experiment:**
- 1 ruler or long stick
- 1 small rock or thick book (fulcrum)
- 1 heavy object to move (book, block, filled water bottle)

**For reading:**
- This lesson (printed or on screen)
- Access to an AI assistant (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or similar)

## Lesson Plan

### Warm-Up (5–10 min)

Ask students:

*"Has anyone ever used a seesaw? What happened when one person was much heavier than the other? What did you have to do to make it balance?"*

Let them discuss. Don't define anything yet. The goal is to surface the intuition — that where you sit on the seesaw matters, that distance from the middle changes something — before the lesson names it.

Then ask: *"Has anyone ever pushed something heavy up a ramp instead of lifting it straight up? Which felt easier?"*

Again, don't explain. Just let them remember.

---

### Main Activity (30–40 min)

The lesson is a story with two built-in pauses. Students read, stop to experiment with their hands, then return to the story. The explanation arrives after the physical experience, not before.

**Read the story below. Stop at each pause and complete the experiment before continuing.**

---

Theo took the long way home.

He did this sometimes, on days that hadn't gone the way he'd hoped. The long way went past the old ravine at the edge of the field, where the ground dropped away suddenly and the light came in sideways in the late afternoon, and everything felt a little older and quieter there. He wasn't sure why that helped. It just did.

He heard her before he saw her.

The sound was low and strange — not an animal sound he recognized, something deeper and slower than that, like the groan of a large door that hadn't been opened in a long time. He stopped walking. His feet stopped before he decided to stop them. That happens sometimes, when something is important.

He came to the edge of the ravine and looked down.

She was enormous. That was the first thing. She was the size of something that shouldn't be real — a great shaggy shape, the color of dark winter grass, with long curved tusks that swept upward like the branches of a tree. A woolly mammoth. Standing in the ravine below him, very still, with the long shadows of late afternoon falling across her back.

She turned her head and looked at him.

Her eye was amber, and old, and patient.

A rock had shifted under her front foot — just a small thing, the way rocks move in autumn when the frost starts working up from below — and now her foot was held fast between the rock and the ground. She had tried everything a mammoth can try alone. The earth around her was disturbed, scraped, marked with effort. And then she had stopped trying, because she was old enough to know when alone isn't working.

She looked at Theo. She did not look afraid. She looked like someone who had been waiting, and had now stopped waiting, because the thing she was waiting for had arrived.

Theo looked at the sky. The sun was already low. He looked at Neva. He looked at his hands.

He had no tools. He had never moved anything the size of a mammoth. He was not sure he could move anything the size of the rock that held her.

But the long shadows were getting longer, and Neva's herd would leave when the dark came, and she was looking at him like he was exactly the right person for this.

She was not still because she was calm. She was still because she was exhausted — and hungry. He could see it in the way she held herself, the way her head hung slightly. She had been here a long time.

There was a walnut tree at the edge of the ravine, branches heavy with the last of the season. Clusters of dark berries clung to the shrubs nearby, the ones that stay through first frost. Food. But the steep side of the ravine dropped almost straight down. He couldn't carry anything down that way without falling.

He looked along the edge until he found it — a long flat piece of bark, torn loose from a fallen tree, wide enough to use. He laid it against the gentler slope of the ravine wall, angling it down toward Neva. Then he piled what he could gather onto it — handfuls of berries, a branch heavy with walnuts — and let the slope do what slopes do.

The food slid down to her. Not gracefully. But it got there.

Neva ate slowly, the way old animals eat. Theo watched from the top of the ravine and waited.

---

*Here is where the story pauses the first time.*

*Before you read what happens next: find a smooth book or a flat object with some weight to it. Find a surface you can tilt — another book, a piece of cardboard, a folder. Make a ramp by propping one end up with something small.*

*Now try this: lift the heavy book straight up off the table. Feel how much effort that takes. Then place it at the bottom of your ramp and slide it up the slope instead. Notice what changes in your hands. Try making the ramp steeper. Try making it shallower.*

*Notice when the sliding gets easier, and when it gets harder. Notice how far you have to push.*

*When you've tried it — come back.*

---

When she finished, Neva raised her head.

She looked at Theo. Her eye was amber, and old, and patient — but something in it had shifted, the way light shifts when a cloud moves. She was still enormous. She was still a mammoth. But she was looking at him differently now.

Like she had decided something.

Theo climbed down into the ravine.

---

Theo looked at what was around him.

There were branches — two of them, one longer than the other, fallen near the ravine wall. There were stones of different sizes. There was the slope of the ravine itself, which was gentler on one side than the other.

He didn't know the word *lever* yet. He just knew that when he was little, he and his cousin had put a plank across a log and jumped on one end and sent each other flying, and something about that lived in his hands as a memory.

He found a stone about the size of his two fists together, and placed it near Neva's trapped foot — close to the rock that held her, not close to him. He took the longer branch and slid one end under the edge of the capturing rock, and rested the branch across his stone.

He pushed down on the other end.

Nothing.

He moved the stone. Closer to the rock, not to himself. Pushed again.

The rock shifted. Just a little. Just enough that Neva felt it, because she shifted too, and made the low slow sound again, and Theo understood she was telling him *yes, that direction, keep going.*

He pushed harder. He leaned his whole weight onto the branch. The rock moved — not far, not dramatically, just enough — and Neva pulled her foot free with a sound like a deep breath.

She stood still for a moment, testing the foot. Then she turned her great head and looked at Theo again.

He looked back at her.

He was breathing hard. His hands were shaking slightly, the way hands do after they've done something they weren't sure they could do. The shadows were very long now. Somewhere beyond the ravine, in the direction she would go, he could almost imagine the sound of the herd.

Neva lowered her head toward him — not all the way, she was too large for that, but enough. A gesture. The kind of thing that means *thank you* in a language older than words.

Then she turned and walked up the gentler slope of the ravine, and into the long light, and away.

---

*Here is where the story pauses the second time.*

*Before you read what happens next: gather what you have. A ruler or a long stick. A rock or a heavy book. A small object to move — an eraser, a block, something with a little weight. A thick book or a wedge to act as a fulcrum.*

*Try to move the heavy object using only your hands. Then try using the stick, with the rock underneath it as a pivot point. Try the stick with the pivot close to the heavy object. Then try the pivot close to your hands.*

*Notice what changes. Notice what your hands feel. Notice when it gets easier, and when it gets harder.*

*When you've tried it — come back. Because now you'll understand what Theo understood, and the story will make a different kind of sense.*

---

Theo sat down on the ground of the ravine. He looked at his hands. He looked at the branch and the stone. He thought about the stone close to the rock, and how that had mattered. He thought about how his whole weight had been necessary, and how it had been enough.

He took the long way home. But it felt different this time. Shorter, somehow.

## What Just Happened in Your Hands

You used two simple machines. You probably didn't call them that. But your hands know what they did.

**The ramp** came first. When Theo laid the bark against the slope and slid the food down to Neva, he was using an inclined plane — a ramp. When you slid a book up your tilted surface instead of lifting it straight up, you felt the same thing he felt: it took less force. Not no force. Less.

Here is why: lifting something straight up fights gravity all at once. A ramp spreads that fight across a longer distance. You push a little at a time, over a longer path, and the total effort in any single moment is smaller. You trade distance for force. The work is the same. The struggle, at any moment, is less.

That is why a gentle slope is easier than a steep one — and why Neva could walk out of the ravine on the gradual side, but not the cliff side.

**The lever** came second. When you used the stick with the pivot underneath it, something changed that was harder to expect.

You made a lever. A lever has three parts: the arm (the stick), the fulcrum (the pivot point underneath), and the load (the thing you're trying to move). When you push down on one end, the fulcrum redirects that force, and the other end pushes up against the load.

Here is the part that surprises most people: where you put the fulcrum changes everything.

When the fulcrum is close to the load — the way Theo moved the stone closer to Neva's rock — a small movement on your end becomes a larger force on the load's end. You trade distance for force again. You push down a long way, and the load moves a short way, but it moves with more power than your hands alone could ever make.

That is what Theo felt when the rock shifted. Not magic. Physics. The branch was multiplying him.

Simple machines don't give you something for nothing. They give you the same work in a more manageable shape.

## AI Integration

Students have now experienced both machines with their hands before any explanation arrived. This is the right order. The AI prompt comes after the physical memory is in place.

**Sample prompts for students:**

*"I slid a heavy book up a ramp instead of lifting it straight up and it felt easier. Why did that happen? What is a ramp actually doing?"*

*"I tried moving a heavy book with a ruler and a smaller book as a fulcrum. When I moved the fulcrum closer to the heavy book, it got easier. Why?"*

*"I helped a woolly mammoth get food using a ramp, and freed her trapped foot using a branch and a stone as a lever. Can you explain the physics of how both of those worked?"*

The AI will explain ramps and levers — mechanical advantage, force and distance, the math underneath what the student's hands already know. It can go as deep as the student wants to go.

**For teachers:** The AI integration is intentionally placed after the physical experiments, not before. Students who ask the AI first will get correct answers they don't yet have the body memory to anchor. Sequence matters.

---

## Wrap-Up / Discussion (5–10 min)

Ask students:

- What surprised you when you moved the fulcrum closer to the heavy object?
- What happened to the ramp when you made it steeper? When you made it shallower?
- Theo traded something to get more force. What did he trade? *(Distance — he had to push farther to move the rock less.)*
- Where else have you seen a ramp or a lever in real life? *(Wheelchair ramps, scissors, bottle openers, seesaws, stairs, skateboard ramps.)*

---

## Assessment

**Exit ticket:** Ask students to draw Theo's lever and label three parts: the arm, the fulcrum, and the load. Then ask them to write one sentence explaining why moving the fulcrum closer to the rock made it easier.

This checks both vocabulary acquisition and conceptual understanding — did they get the mechanism, not just the word?

---

## Differentiation

**Support:** For students who struggle with the reading, the teacher can read the story aloud and pause together for the experiments. The physical component is the same regardless of reading level.

**Extension:** Ask students to find three examples of levers or ramps in the school building and sketch them, labeling arm, fulcrum, and load (for levers) or height, length, and slope (for ramps). Then ask: what was traded in each case?

**ELL/Bilingual:** Key vocabulary with Spanish translations:
- Simple machine / Máquina simple
- Ramp / Rampa
- Lever / Palanca
- Fulcrum / Punto de apoyo
- Force / Fuerza
- Load / Carga
- Mechanical advantage / Ventaja mecánica

The story is written with simple sentence structures and concrete imagery throughout, which supports comprehension for English language learners.

---

## Notes from the Classroom

This lesson was designed around a specific sequencing principle: hands before vocabulary, discovery before explanation. The two story pauses are not optional interruptions — they are the lesson. If students skip the experiments and read straight through, they will understand the story but miss the point.

The AI prompt works best when students arrive at it with a question that came from their own hands. "Why did that happen?" is a different question when you already felt something happen.

Neva is patient. She can wait for the explanation. So can the vocabulary.
