# Your Assessment System Can't See Learning

*And that was true before AI made it worse.*

---

When I first started teaching after grad school, I was walking the hall one day. I stopped to listen to a kid explaining the water cycle, and I remember thinking: this is the problem.

He could tell me everything. He was doing something with his hands while he talked (most likely from being nervous, or just needing to move), and the explanation came out clean and connected and precise. Evaporation. Condensation. Why precipitation happens and not just "it rains." He used the vocabulary correctly. He traced causation. He got it.

I later found out he hadn't turned in a worksheet all semester.

Which one was the assessment? I kept thinking about that. Not as a rhetorical question but as an actual one. What was I supposed to do with what I'd just observed? I knew something about this kid's understanding that the gradebook didn't know and, under normal circumstances, couldn't know. The gap between those two things bothered me more the longer I thought about it.

---

The problem I kept running into wasn't new, and it wasn't about AI. It was older and simpler: assessment systems are built to see output, not learning. They ask whether a student produced a thing in a format at a time, and then they report the answer as though it was a measurement of understanding.

Most of the time those questions track together. Most students who know something can show it in the expected way when asked. But the students who fall into the gap between what they know and what they can produce in the format: that population is not random. It falls on predictable lines. Processing speed. Language fluency. How safe the room feels. Whether the pathway the test offers happens to be the one this particular brain uses.

I watched a fourth-grader demonstrate this during a living history museum day inside the classroom. She was portraying a historical composer, had prepared a whole performance, brought a keyboard. When she got to her spot there was no working outlet. The setup she'd built required the instrument.

What she did: she sang the thematic material and simultaneously mimed the keyboard. Both channels at once. Under constraint. Without being prompted to adapt.

The content was all there: the musical knowledge, the historical knowledge, integrated and visible in a form no written assessment would have asked for. I remember watching it and thinking: that's mastery. That's exactly what mastery looks like. And I also remember thinking: if this had happened on a test day instead of a museum day, we'd have counted it as nothing.

At the same event, a fifth-grader who was typically logged as unfocused (easily distracted, often off-task, the kind of kid whose daily presence in a classroom reads as a problem to be managed) stayed in character for the full interaction window. Recalled specific dates without looking at his notes. Spontaneously connected a date from a pre-event calendar discussion to the birth year of the historical figure he was portraying. That's transfer. That's the actual target of the lesson. It came out in a format his seat-work record never would have predicted.

I've seen enough versions of this that I stopped thinking of them as exceptions. They're the rule. Students express understanding through more pathways than the worksheet knows to look for. Embodied: the kid who needs to move to explain. Performative: the one who needs the scaffold of a role before the knowledge surfaces. Delayed: the one for whom the answer arrives overnight and nowhere else. Verbal and non-written: the one who can tell you everything and can't hold the pencil still long enough to write it down. Narrative: the one who can explain a concept fluently if you let them build a story around it, and goes silent when asked for a direct response.

These aren't learning differences, exactly. They're evidence differences. The understanding is there. The pathway we're looking at isn't wide enough to see it.

---

When AI tools arrived in classrooms, most of the conversation went in one direction: how do we know the output is theirs? Proctoring software, revised honor codes, detection tools. The whole discourse organized itself around output integrity.

I understood the concern. But I kept thinking it was addressing the wrong thing.

The visible-output problem already existed. AI made output cheaper and faster and more polished. The student who was already producing work without deep understanding could now produce more of it. The student who understood things deeply but struggled with the format: nothing changed for that kid. Nothing at all. The intervention landscape didn't shift for him. The gradebook still couldn't see him.

There's a version of "how do we know the output is theirs" that's a real question worth asking. But it assumes that verified output equals verified understanding. It doesn't. You can confirm the provenance of an artifact and still know nothing about whether the student learned anything. The worksheet kid in the hallway: his worksheets, the ones he didn't turn in, those were authentically his. He's not a cheating problem. He's a visibility problem. And the AI-detection frame has nothing useful to say about him.

---

I want to be careful here about what I'm arguing for.

I'm not saying standards should come down. I'm not saying a verbal conversation in the hallway is always sufficient evidence. I'm not saying teachers should be making high-stakes decisions on informally observed moments or that any demonstration is as valid as any other.

I'm saying the format of a demonstration is not the same as the fact of understanding. And when a system treats them as equivalent (when only certain formats count, in only certain windows, under only certain conditions), it generates false negatives at scale. A student who understands something gets counted as not understanding it. They get intervention they don't need, remediation that doesn't fit, a story about themselves that isn't true. That story compounds.

And the students most likely to generate false negatives aren't distributed evenly. They're the students who were already most likely to be misread.

---

The piece of this I've thought about most is teacher judgment, because I think it's where the whole thing lives or dies.

A teacher who has been watching a specific student for months knows things about how that student's understanding surfaces that no rubric captures. Which student needs to pace before they can explain. Which one performs better when the audience feels real. Which one's oral fluency has always significantly outpaced their written production and probably always will. That knowledge isn't soft. It's not a feeling. It's professional expertise accumulated through sustained observation, and it's some of the most accurate assessment data in the building.

The problem is it can't travel. "I know she understands it": the teacher has done the hardest work, the actual noticing and tracking and interpreting, and then can't communicate it in terms that hold up institutionally. It gets classified as subjective. Impressionistic. Insufficient.

What it needs is language. Not new technology. Language: shared, specific, calibrated across a faculty. Not "I just knew she understood it," which is true but not useful to a parent or an administrator or a record. But: *the student accurately represented the core concept through non-written expression* or *the student demonstrated transfer by connecting current material to prior work without prompting* or *the student's verbal explanation contained accurate technical vocabulary and logical sequencing aligned to the learning goal.*

That language describes what was observed. Not what was inferred about the student's nature, not a characterization of their capacity, but what happened, in specific terms, against a specific standard. Building that language together across a staff is a calibration practice, the same kind of calibration that already makes writing assessment reliable and defensible across classrooms. It's infrastructure. It's not easy, but it's also not mysterious.

---

AI belongs in this, but in one specific place: after engagement.

Not as the first thing a student reaches for when they're confused. Not as the thing that produces the artifact. After the student has grappled with the material, tried to explain something, noticed where the explanation broke down, then AI can be a useful scaffold for articulation. For finding written language for something already understood. For the student who knows something embodied or verbally but hasn't yet built the written vocabulary for it, AI used after real engagement can be a legitimate bridge.

The sequencing is everything. Engagement and sense-making first. Tool support second. This isn't distrust: it's what actually builds durable understanding, and it also makes the tool's output mean something rather than nothing.

Transparency about when and how AI is appropriate is a governance question, not a surveillance question. The difference matters to me. Surveillance assumes bad faith and monitors for it. Governance assumes legitimate use and structures it. Teaching students when a tool serves their thinking and when it substitutes for it: that's the actual skill. It's assessable. It's worth teaching directly.

---

I think about that kid in the hallway a lot.

He understood the water cycle. He understood it well, in a way I'd verified by accident because we happened to be standing in the same place at the same time and he happened to be talking. What the gradebook knew about him was different. And what the gradebook knew about him was what would follow him: into the next grade, into the conversation with his parents, into his own story about what he's good at.

The fix isn't oral exams for everyone or abandoning written work. It's building systems that are more sensitive to the actual signal: more pathways, documented in shared language, evaluated against the same goals. It's treating teacher observation as evidence when it's grounded in specific documented demonstration. It's asking, honestly, whether the assessment you're running is likely to generate false negatives, and for which students, and whether that's acceptable.

The question isn't *did this student produce output in this format*. It's *what does this student's understanding look like, and how do I know?*

The second question is harder. It requires more from teachers, more institutional investment in shared language, more willingness to sit with the complexity of what assessment is actually trying to do.

It is also more accurate. And the students it would stop miscounting: they've been waiting a long time for the system to get more accurate.
