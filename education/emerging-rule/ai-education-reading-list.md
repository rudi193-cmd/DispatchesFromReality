# AI and Education — Reading List for Teachers

**For:** [Emerging-Rule/community](https://github.com/Emerging-Rule/community) · Issue #4  
**Curator:** Professor Archimedes Oakenscroll (UTETY, Department of Numerical Ethics & Accidental Cosmology)  
**Editorial standard:** *Posole criterion* — each source must work for a teacher with thirty students and no prep period; sources that admit what they do not know are preferred over performative comprehensiveness.  
**Count:** 10 sources (all free at time of listing)  
**ΔΣ=42**

Annotations below are one paragraph each: why this source belongs on the list, and what it honestly does not resolve.

---

## 1. UNESCO — Guidance for generative AI in education and research (2023)

**URL:** https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/guidance-generative-ai-education-and-research  
**Format:** Report (PDF available from UNESCO)

This belongs first because it is policy written for people who must govern tools they did not build — which is the actual condition of most K–12 teachers in 2026. UNESCO names age limits, data privacy, and a human-centred validation process rather than pretending the teacher becomes a prompt engineer overnight. It admits openly that regulatory frameworks lag behind product releases and that institutions are largely unprepared to validate GenAI tools — which is precisely the honest starting posture a reading list should model. It will not tell you which app to install on Tuesday; it will tell you what questions to ask before you do. *Posole:* usable as a staff-meeting handout; pair with one classroom activity from AI4K12, not read cover-to-cover under fluorescent lights.

---

## 2. U.S. Department of Education — Artificial Intelligence and the Future of Teaching and Learning (2023)

**URL:** https://www.ed.gov/sites/ed/files/documents/ai-report/ai-report.pdf  
**Format:** Report (public domain) · **Summary:** https://tech.ed.gov/ai/

Filed here because it defines AI for educators as *automation based on associations* — a definition humble enough to survive contact with a twelve-year-old who thinks the machine is always right. The report’s insistence on *humans-in-the-loop* is not sentiment; it is a governance requirement, and it maps directly to the question beneath K–12 AI policy: how does the adult remain the adult when the system speaks with tenure-committee confidence? It admits that pattern detection and automated instructional decisions can introduce bias and unfairness, and that educational systems must govern delegation — it does not claim the tools are neutral. It is U.S.-centric and pre-dates the latest model generation; treat it as structural ethics, not a product guide. *Posole:* the seven core recommendations fit on one printed page if you use the Department’s core-messages handout.

---

## 3. UNESCO — AI and education: guidance for policy-makers (2021)

**URL:** https://doi.org/10.54675/pcsp7350  
**Format:** Report (UNESCO Digital Library)

Included because the 2023 GenAI guidance assumes you already understand that *education policy* and *tool adoption* are different problems wearing similar hats. The 2021 document is shorter, older, and more explicit about equity, inclusion, and the gap between what countries promise and what classrooms receive. It admits that AI in education is unevenly distributed and that policy must address preparation of teachers — not merely procurement of software. It will feel dated on large language models; read it for the epistemology of policy, then read #1 for GenAI specifics. *Posole:* one sitting; useful before a district committee meeting where someone will conflate “AI strategy” with “buy ChatGPT Plus for everyone.”

---

## 4. AI4K12 — Five Big Ideas in Artificial Intelligence

**URL:** https://ai4k12.org/  
**Poster:** https://ai4k12.org/resources/big-ideas-poster/  
**Format:** Framework, posters, grade-band progressions (Creative Commons)

This is the spine. Perception, representation and reasoning, learning, natural interaction, societal impact — five ideas that give a teacher language without Lagrangians. AI4K12 admits, by structure, that K–12 AI education is still being invented: the guidelines were draft progressions, the resource directory is curated not complete, and the initiative explicitly invites local adaptation under CC license. That is the correct honesty for a field in motion. It does not replace your math curriculum; it gives you a map so you do not teach “AI” as synonymous with “chatbot.” *Posole:* print the poster; teach one big idea per week; do not attempt the entire directory in one semester unless you dislike sleep.

---

## 5. AI4K12 — Activity Resource Guides

**URL:** https://ai4k12.org/activities/  
**Format:** Lesson-style guides (CC licensed)

Filed separately from the framework because teachers asked for something they could *do* on a Thursday afternoon, and the guides answer that without requiring a district contract. SpeechDemo, Markov chains, diffusion demos — each ties a concrete activity to a big idea and admits, in the Markov and diffusion materials especially, that large models are *more complex* versions of ideas students can touch with simpler tools. The guides do not claim universal grade fit; you must match activity to your students. *Posole:* pick one guide, one class period; prerequisites are documented; failure is instructional data, not moral failure.

---

## 6. MIT RAISE — Day of AI

**URL:** https://dayofai.org/  
**Format:** Free teacher professional development + grade-banded curriculum (annual event + materials)

Included because it is designed around the constraint real schools have: one day, one coherent arc, teachers who have never taught AI before. The program admits it is an entry point, not a full CS pathway — which is exactly what most homeroom and elective teachers need before they commit to a semester. Materials are tied to AI literacy rather than vocational ML engineering. It will not resolve your district’s Acceptable Use Policy; it assumes you will handle governance elsewhere (#1, #2). *Posole:* high; built for the teacher with no prep period who still wants students to leave having *done* something once.

## 7. Laupichler et al. — AI literacy in K-12: a systematic literature review (2023)

**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1186/s40594-023-00418-7  
**Format:** Open-access journal article (*International Journal of STEM Education*)

This belongs on a teacher reading list not because every teacher will read sixty pages of literature review, but because *someone* in the building should — and this source models how to hold “AI literacy” as a contested term rather than a marketing phrase. The review admits fragmentation in definitions, uneven empirical bases, and gaps between what researchers measure and what classrooms need. That admission is the bibliography doing its job: it tells you the field is young and the confident vendor deck is lying. Assign to your department lead or union rep; excerpt definitions for staff PD. *Posole:* low for daily lesson planning; high for preventing performative comprehensiveness at the district level.

---

## 8. Touretzky et al. — A differentiated discussion about AI education in K-12 (2021)

**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s13218-021-00724-8  
**Format:** Journal article (AI4K12 leadership; check institutional access — often available via author pages or ResearchGate)

Filed because it draws a line between *AI appreciation*, *AI literacy*, and *AI skill* — a distinction teachers need when a parent asks why students are not “learning Python for ChatGPT.” It admits that not every student needs the same depth, and that conflating goals produces both bored fifth-graders and under-prepared ninth-graders. It predates generative AI’s classroom explosion; read it for taxonomy, not for tool recommendations. *Posole:* one department discussion; stops the malformed question “should we teach AI?” from being answered with a single syllabus.

---

## 9. Touretzky & Gardner-McCune — Machine Learning and the Five Big Ideas in AI (2023)

**URL:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s40593-022-00314-1  
**Format:** Journal article (open access via publisher)

Included because it connects the Five Big Ideas (#4) to machine learning in language a secondary teacher can defend to a skeptical principal. It admits that ML is only one branch of AI, that societal impact must be taught alongside technique, and that teacher preparation remains the bottleneck — none of which is flattering to edtech sales decks, which is why it is useful. It will not replace hands-on activities (#5); it explains *why* the activities are structured as they are. *Posole:* read before you argue for an AI unit in science vs. CS vs. homeroom; saves a malformed curriculum map.

---

## 10. Common Sense Education — AI literacy for educators and families

**URL:** https://www.commonsense.org/education/ai-literacy  
**Format:** Articles, lessons, family tips (free web resources)

Filed last not because it is least important but because it is the most likely to be *overused* — short, friendly, and dangerously easy to mistake for completeness. Common Sense admits, in tone if not always in headline, that families and teachers face different risks: privacy at home, pedagogy at school, hype everywhere. Use it for conversation starters and parent nights; do not treat it as your only ethics framework when UNESCO and the Department of Education exist (#1, #2). *Posole:* excellent for a 20-minute homeroom or parent email; insufficient alone for a district policy debate.

---

## Optional eleventh — TeachAI — Guidance for Schools Toolkit

**URL:** https://www.teachai.org/  
**Format:** Toolkit, checklists (multi-organization partnership)

Listed as optional because it is newer and more checklist-shaped than the UNESCO/Department anchors — useful when a principal asks “what do we do Monday?” but thinner on epistemology. It admits, implicitly, that schools need guardrails before they need more apps. Include if your audience is administrators; omit if you need ten sources exactly and already have #1–#2 for governance.

---

## What this list deliberately excludes

- **Spanish-only curriculum translation** — wrong kind of expertise; see Emerging Rule issue #2; do not touch without a teacher who teaches in Spanish.
- **Vendor white papers** — performative comprehensiveness; fails posole.
- **“AI will transform education” preprints** — usually neither citable nor honest about limits.
- **Federal Register priority rules** — for grant lawyers, not homeroom teachers.
